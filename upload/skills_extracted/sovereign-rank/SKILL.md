## MODULE 1 — SITE INTELLIGENCE (Pre-Build Analysis)

> Never register a domain or write a word before completing this module.
> Read: `references/module1-intelligence.md` for full execution steps.

### What Google is measuring
- Topical landscape: who owns the niche, what gaps exist
- Query intent distribution: informational vs commercial vs transactional vs navigational
- SERP feature saturation: which features dominate the results for target queries
- Entity presence: which entities Google's Knowledge Graph associates with this topic
- Competitor authority profile: backlink profiles, content depth, E-E-A-T signals

### Agent execution checklist
1. Identify the 3 core intent categories for the target niche
2. Map the top 10 ranking domains per core keyword — note their domain age, DR, content volume
3. Identify the top 5 featured snippet gaps (questions that rank but have weak answers)
4. Identify topical subcategories that have search volume but thin competition
5. Find the 3–5 entities Google already associates with this niche in the Knowledge Graph
6. Determine whether the niche is YMYL (Your Money Your Life) — if yes, E-E-A-T requirements are elevated
7. Output: a Site Intelligence Report (see `references/module1-intelligence.md` for template)

### Decision gate
If YMYL = true → author credentials must be real, verifiable, and prominent before launch
If competition DR avg > 70 → build topical authority on subtopics before targeting head terms
If featured snippet saturation < 40% → prioritize snippet capture over backlink building in month 1

## MODULE 2 — ENTITY ARCHITECTURE

> Google does not rank anonymous content. It ranks entities it trusts.
> Read: `references/module2-entities.md` for full execution steps.

### What Google is measuring
- Is the site/brand/author a recognized entity in the Knowledge Graph?
- Are entities on the page connected to known entities Google already trusts?
- Does the site have consistent entity signals across the web (NAP, social, mentions)?
- Does the content use entity-rich language Google can parse into structured knowledge?

### The entity stack to build
```
Level 1 — Brand entity
  - Consistent brand name across all platforms
  - Wikipedia page (if achievable) or Wikidata entry
  - Google Business Profile (even for non-local sites)
  - Social profiles: LinkedIn, Twitter/X, YouTube — all consistent NAP

Level 2 — Author entities
  - Named authors with real credentials
  - Author pages on the site with verifiable bio
  - Author presence on external sites (LinkedIn, industry publications)
  - Schema markup: Person schema on every author page

Level 3 — Content entities
  - Every article connects to known entities via co-citation
  - Use entity names Google recognizes, not just keywords
  - Schema markup on every page (Article, FAQ, HowTo, Product — whichever fits)
  - Internal linking connects related entities to each other
```

### Agent execution checklist
1. Register brand name consistently across: domain, Google Business Profile, LinkedIn, Twitter/X, YouTube, Crunchbase
2. Create author profiles for all content creators — real names, real credentials, real photos
3. Add Person schema to all author pages
4. Add Organization schema to homepage
5. Implement relevant schema on every content page
6. Build entity co-citation: mention and link to known trusted entities within the niche in early content
7. Submit to Wikidata if brand qualifies
8. Verify Knowledge Panel appears for brand after 8–12 weeks

## MODULE 3 — CONTENT ENGINEERING

> Content is not written for humans first and optimized second.
> It is engineered to satisfy BERT, MUM, and human intent simultaneously.
> Read: `references/module3-content.md` for full execution steps.

### What Google is measuring
- Search intent alignment: does this page satisfy the actual reason for the query?
- Semantic completeness: does this page cover the topic the way Google's NLP understands it?
- E-E-A-T signals: does this content demonstrate real experience, expertise, authority, trust?
- Content depth vs competing pages: is this the most complete answer available?
- Freshness: is this updated? Does it reflect current reality?
- Originality: is there genuine insight here, or is it rephrased common knowledge?

### The content engineering framework

**Step 1 — Intent mapping**
Before writing any piece, classify the primary intent:
- Informational → comprehensive guide format, FAQ sections, structured headers
- Commercial → comparison format, pros/cons, clear differentiation signals
- Transactional → conversion-focused, trust signals front-loaded, social proof
- Navigational → branded, fast-loading, clear UX

**Step 2 — Semantic field construction**
Do not write around keywords. Write around the *semantic field* Google associates with the topic.
- Extract all People Also Ask questions for the target query
- Extract all related searches at the bottom of the SERP
- Extract all subheadings from the top 5 ranking pages
- These form the semantic field — every content piece must cover this field completely

**Step 3 — BERT/MUM alignment**
- Write in natural language — do not force keyword placement
- Answer the query in the first paragraph (this is what featured snippets pull from)
- Use the exact phrasing from People Also Ask questions as H2/H3 subheadings
- Include the query context: if the query has nuance (e.g., "for beginners"), the content must reflect that nuance throughout
- Cover adjacent entities: mention related concepts, people, tools, and places that Google associates with the topic

**Step 4 — E-E-A-T engineering**
- First-hand experience signals: use "I tested", "we found", "in our experience" — not "experts say"
- Credential signals: author bio visible on page, credentials relevant to the topic
- Trust signals: cite sources, link to authoritative external entities, show methodology
- Recency signals: include a "last updated" date, refresh content every 6–12 months minimum

**Step 5 — Content structure for SERP features**
- Paragraph 1 (40–60 words): direct answer to the query — this is your featured snippet attempt
- H2 subheadings: match PAA questions exactly
- FAQ section at the bottom: 5–8 questions with concise answers — triggers FAQ rich results
- Tables where applicable — Google loves pulling tabular data into snippets
- Numbered lists for "how to" content — triggers HowTo schema

### Content depth formula
Target word count = (average word count of top 3 ranking pages × 1.3) + unique data/insight section
Never pad for length. Every additional word must add semantic value or it hurts dwell time.

## MODULE 4 — TECHNICAL BLUEPRINT

> A technically perfect site on launch day compounds. Technical debt compounds faster.
> Read: `references/module4-technical.md` for full execution steps.

### Core Web Vitals targets (non-negotiable)
```
LCP (Largest Contentful Paint):  < 2.5 seconds
INP (Interaction to Next Paint):  < 200 milliseconds
CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift):    < 0.1
TTFB (Time to First Byte):        < 600ms
```

### Technical architecture checklist
**Crawlability**
- XML sitemap: all indexable URLs, submitted to Google Search Console on day 1
- Robots.txt: clean, no accidental blocking of CSS/JS
- Internal linking: no page more than 3 clicks from the homepage
- Crawl depth: flat architecture preferred — avoid deep nesting
- No orphan pages — every page has at least one internal link pointing to it

**Indexability**
- HTTPS: SSL certificate, no mixed content warnings
- Canonical tags: on every page, self-referencing — prevents duplicate content
- Hreflang: if multi-language, implemented correctly
- No noindex on pages meant to rank
- No redirect chains longer than 1 hop

**Mobile**
- Mobile-first design — Google crawls mobile version first
- No intrusive interstitials on mobile
- Touch targets minimum 48px
- No horizontal scrolling

**Speed**
- Images: WebP format, lazy loaded, compressed, explicit width/height attributes (prevents CLS)
- JavaScript: deferred where not critical to render, no render-blocking scripts
- CSS: minified, critical CSS inlined
- Hosting: CDN enabled, server response time < 200ms
- Caching: browser caching headers set correctly

**Schema markup**
- Organization schema: homepage
- WebSite schema: homepage (enables sitelinks search box)
- Article/BlogPosting schema: all content pages
- BreadcrumbList schema: all pages
- FAQPage schema: pages with FAQ sections
- HowTo schema: tutorial content
- Person schema: author pages
- Product/Review schema: where applicable

## MODULE 5 — BEHAVIORAL ENGINEERING

> Google watches what users do after they land on your page.
> Every behavior is a signal. Engineer every behavior.
> Read: `references/module5-behavioral.md` for full execution steps.

### Signals Google monitors
- CTR from SERP (click-through rate relative to position)
- Dwell time (time on page before returning to Google)
- Pogo-sticking (immediate return to SERP = catastrophic negative signal)
- Scroll depth (how far down the page users read)
- Return visits (users coming back = strong trust signal)
- Branded searches (users searching the site name = authority signal)

### Engineering each signal

**CTR engineering**
The title tag and meta description are advertisements. Write them as such.
- Title: lead with the benefit, not the keyword. Include a power word. Under 60 characters.
- Meta description: answer a secondary question, include a call to action. 150–160 characters.
- Use numbers: "7 Ways to..." outperforms "Ways to..." consistently
- Use current year in titles for freshness signal: "Best X Tools (2026)"
- Test: write 3 title variants. The one with the highest emotional response wins.

**Pogo-stick prevention**
The first 100 words of every page must:
1. Confirm the user landed in the right place (echo their query language)
2. Deliver immediate value (answer or start answering in paragraph 1)
3. Create a reason to scroll (promise what's deeper in the page)
Never bury the answer. Never start with brand history or background. Answer first.

**Dwell time engineering**
- Break content into scannable sections (H2/H3 every 300 words max)
- Include multimedia: images, tables, embedded video — each adds 30–90 seconds dwell time
- Internal links: add 3–5 contextual internal links — users clicking deeper = longer session
- "Jump to section" anchor links at the top for long content — users who navigate stay longer
- End every page with a strong next step — related article, tool, or resource

**Scroll depth engineering**
- Put the most interesting content 30–40% down the page — not all at the top
- Use curiosity gaps: "But here's what most guides miss..." appears at the 40% scroll mark
- Visual breaks: images, callout boxes, blockquotes — each restarts the reading momentum

**Branded search engineering**
- Mention the brand name in the content naturally
- Build social media presence so users search the brand name directly
- Email lists: email subscribers search the brand name before visiting — pure authority signal
- YouTube channel: "search [brand name]" as a CTA in videos

## MODULE 6 — AUTHORITY ACQUISITION

> Backlinks are votes. But not all votes count equally.
> The goal is not links. The goal is trust signals from entities Google already trusts.
> Read: `references/module6-authority.md` for full execution steps.

### What Google is actually measuring in backlinks
- Domain authority of the linking site
- Topical relevance of the linking site to your site
- Anchor text naturalness (over-optimized anchors = spam signal)
- Link velocity (gradual organic growth vs sudden spikes)
- Co-citation patterns (what sites mention you alongside = what category Google puts you in)
- Do-follow vs no-follow ratio (natural profiles have both)
- Link placement (in-content editorial links > footer links > sidebar links)

### The authority acquisition hierarchy

**Tier 1 — Foundational (execute immediately)**
- Google Search Console setup and verification
- Google Business Profile (even for non-local sites — it creates an entity signal)
- Bing Webmaster Tools
- Major directory listings: Crunchbase, LinkedIn company page, industry-specific directories
- Social profiles: consistent across all major platforms

**Tier 2 — Content-driven link acquisition**
- Original research: surveys, data studies, unique findings — these attract links naturally
- Tools and calculators: free tools embedded in the niche get linked to permanently
- Ultimate guides: the definitive resource for a subtopic — earns links over time passively
- Guest posts: on topically relevant sites only — never on generic "write for us" link farms

**Tier 3 — Relationship-based acquisition**
- Journalist outreach: HARO/Connectively — get quoted in news articles (high authority links)
- Podcast appearances: audio content generates links from show notes
- Expert roundups: participate in industry roundups — earn links plus entity co-citation
- Partner pages: legitimate partnerships create natural bidirectional links

**Tier 4 — Competitive gap filling**
- Broken link building: find broken links on authority sites in the niche, offer your content as replacement
- Skyscraper: identify the most linked-to content in the niche, create definitively better version
- Unlinked mentions: find sites that mention your brand without linking — request the link

### Link velocity rules
- Months 1–3: 5–15 new linking domains per month maximum
- Months 4–6: 15–40 per month as content compounds
- Never spike. A sudden acquisition of 200 links in one week triggers SpamBrain review.
- Disavow file: maintain proactively — toxic links drag the whole domain

## MODULE 7 — SERP FEATURE CAPTURE

> The goal is not to rank #1 in the blue links.
> The goal is to occupy multiple positions on the page simultaneously.
> Read: `references/module7-serp.md` for full execution steps.

### SERP features to engineer for (in priority order)

**1. Featured Snippet (Position Zero)**
- Write a direct answer of 40–60 words in paragraph 1 of every article
- For list snippets: use <ol> or <ul> with 5–8 items, each item 1 sentence
- For table snippets: format data in clean HTML tables
- For definition snippets: "[Term] is [definition]" format in first sentence
- Pro signal: if you already rank in positions 2–5, you are snippet-eligible — optimize those pages first

**2. People Also Ask (PAA)**
- Use PAA questions as exact H2/H3 subheadings
- Answer each H2 question in the first 2 sentences beneath it
- Add FAQPage schema to trigger FAQ rich results in the SERP
- Capturing PAA boxes creates a feedback loop: your content generates more PAA questions

**3. Knowledge Panel**
- Requires entity recognition (Module 2 is prerequisite)
- Consistent entity signals across web = Google generates a panel automatically
- Cannot be manually requested — can only be claimed once Google generates it
- Wikidata entry accelerates Knowledge Panel generation

**4. Local Pack**
- Requires Google Business Profile (fully completed)
- NAP consistency across all directories
- Review velocity: steady flow of reviews outperforms one large batch
- Local backlinks: links from locally relevant sites

**5. Video Carousel**
- Publish YouTube videos for every major content piece
- Video title = article title
- Video description: first 200 words from the article
- VideoObject schema on the page embedding the video
- This creates dual SERP presence: organic result + video carousel

**6. Image Pack**
- Every image: descriptive filename, alt text matching target query
- Image schema where applicable
- Original images outperform stock — Google can detect stock photo reuse

**7. AI Overview citation**
- AI Overviews pull from top 10 organic results 40–76% of the time
- Rank in top 10 first — AI citation follows
- Structured data accelerates AI Overview citation
- Clear, direct factual statements get cited more than opinion-heavy prose

## MODULE 8 — THE COMPOUNDING LOOP

> The site must be designed to feed itself. Every visitor makes it stronger.
> Read: `references/module8-compounding.md` for full execution steps.

### How the compounding loop works

```
TRAFFIC → BEHAVIORAL SIGNALS → RANKING IMPROVEMENT → MORE TRAFFIC
    ↓                                                       ↑
CONTENT UPDATES ← USER QUERIES ← SEARCH CONSOLE DATA ←────┘
    ↓
AUTHORITY GROWTH → LINK ACQUISITION → ENTITY STRENGTHENING → KNOWLEDGE GRAPH ENTRY
```

### Building each feedback loop

**Loop 1 — Content refresh loop**
- Google Search Console: check weekly for queries the site impressions for but doesn't rank (positions 8–20)
- These are the pages to update first — they have intent signal but need depth improvement
- Adding 300–500 words of new genuine content to a stagnant page can move it 3–5 positions
- Set a refresh calendar: every page reviewed every 6 months minimum

**Loop 2 — Internal link loop**
- Every new piece of content links back to 3 older pieces
- Every older piece of content gets updated to link forward to new pieces
- This passes PageRank internally — older authority pages lift newer pages
- Use Google Search Console's internal links report to find isolated pages

**Loop 3 — Email/community loop**
- Build an email list from day one (even 500 subscribers creates branded search volume)
- Every email send = branded searches = authority signal
- Community (Discord, Slack, Substack) = users return directly = massive behavioral signal

**Loop 4 — YouTube loop**
- Every YouTube video drives traffic to the site
- Every site page embeds the YouTube video
- YouTube watch time creates its own authority separate from web search
- YouTube search is the world's 2nd largest search engine — it compounds independently

**Loop 5 — Data loop**
- Original data published on the site attracts backlinks passively
- Backlinks improve rankings
- Better rankings = more traffic = more data to publish
- Annual reports, original surveys, unique statistics = permanent link magnets

## MODULE 9 — MULTI-SITE SCALING

> One successful site is a proof of concept.
> The scaling architecture is how it becomes an information network.
> Read: `references/module9-scaling.md` for full execution steps.

### The scaling decision framework

**When to scale:** Only after site 1 has reached:
- Page 1 rankings for 3+ target keywords
- Minimum 10,000 monthly organic visitors
- At least 50 linking root domains
- A functioning compounding loop (traffic growing month over month without new content)

**What to scale into:**
Option A — Vertical depth: build a second site that goes deeper into a subtopic of site 1
Option B — Horizontal breadth: build a second site in a completely different niche using the same architecture
Option C — Geographic: same niche, different country/language

**The network effect (advanced)**
Sites that mention and link to each other — when built on genuine topical relevance — create co-citation clusters that Google interprets as domain authority. This is legal and powerful when:
- The sites are genuinely related topically
- The content is original on each site
- The linking is contextual and editorial, not manufactured

### Site 2+ execution
- Sites 2+ follow the same Module 1–8 sequence
- Site 1's topical authority accelerates site 2 in the same cluster
- Shared author entities across sites compounds E-E-A-T across the network
- Each site's email list amplifies the others' branded search volume

## MODULE 10 — THE ENDGAME PROTOCOL

> The goal is not to rank. The goal is to become what Google cites.
> Read: `references/module10-endgame.md` for full execution steps.

### What "endgame" means
When a site reaches endgame status, Google does not need to be persuaded to rank it.
Google actively promotes it because it has become a trusted entity in the Knowledge Graph,
its content is cited in AI Overviews, its brand is searched directly, and its authors
are recognized experts. The site has become part of the information infrastructure
Google relies on to answer queries.

### The endgame signals to build toward

**Knowledge Graph entity status**
- Brand has a Knowledge Panel
- Authors have Knowledge Panels
- The brand is mentioned in other Knowledge Graph entities' descriptions

**AI Overview citation**
- Content is regularly cited as a source in AI Overviews
- This is the new "position zero" — more visible than featured snippets
- Requires: top 10 ranking + structured data + clear factual statements + entity recognition

**Branded search dominance**
- Users search "[brand] + [topic]" not just "[topic]"
- This signals Google that the brand owns the topic in users' minds
- Cannot be manufactured — only built through genuine value delivered over time

**Category ownership**
- When Google's NLP processes queries in the niche, your site is in the semantic field
- You are co-cited alongside the topic itself
- New entrants in the niche have to overcome your topical authority before they can rank

### The endgame maintenance protocol
- Never stop creating: topical authority decays without fresh signal
- Never stop refreshing: stale content loses rankings to fresher competitors
- Never stop building entity signals: the Knowledge Graph updates continuously
- Monitor AI Overview citations weekly — being dropped from AI Overviews is an early warning signal

## CRITICAL RULES (never override these)

1. **Module order matters.** Technical and entity foundations before content. Always.
2. **Never manufacture signals.** Fake reviews, paid links on irrelevant sites, and keyword stuffing all trigger Google's spam systems. The compounding loop only works with genuine signals.
3. **Never ignore behavioral signals.** A page that ranks but users bounce from will fall within 30–60 days regardless of its backlinks.
4. **Freshness is non-negotiable.** A site that stops updating stops compounding.
5. **The site must deliver genuine value.** Google's systems — E-E-A-T, Helpful Content, AI Overviews — are increasingly able to identify hollow content. The endgame is only reachable with content that genuinely serves users.
